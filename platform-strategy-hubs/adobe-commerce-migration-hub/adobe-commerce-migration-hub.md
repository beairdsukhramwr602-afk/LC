# Adobe Commerce Migration Hub

Adobe Commerce is an enterprise commerce target for businesses whose migration planning depends on more than storefront flexibility.

It shares core structural foundations with Magento, but it becomes a distinct destination when the future store must support native B2B company structure, shared catalogs with differentiated product access and pricing, broader merchandising and campaign control, and a heavier governance model across teams, storefront contexts, and business rules. In migration terms, the question is not only whether products, customers, and orders can be moved. The more important question is whether Adobe Commerce can preserve the commercial logic that makes the store work.

For many enterprise migrations, that logic sits inside company accounts, pricing visibility, customer-group behavior, staged content and promotional changes, scope-aware storefront structure, and the interaction between multiple commercial contexts. When those layers matter, Adobe Commerce should be judged by behavioral continuity and operating fit, not by the appearance of a successful import.

The shared Magento baseline still applies. Adobe Commerce remains a platform with native product-type structure, attributes and attribute sets, websites / stores / store views hierarchy, customer groups, and native URL rewrites. What changes is the added weight of native B2B structures, shared-catalog governance, staged content operations, and a broader validation surface across business and consumer use cases.

### What Makes Adobe Commerce Different from Magento

A central distinction is native B2B structure.

Adobe Commerce supports companies and shared catalogs, allowing the target to represent business-customer relationships more directly. Shared catalogs can govern product access and custom pricing for different companies, and Adobe Commerce documents that creating a shared catalog also creates a corresponding customer group. That makes the target much stronger when the commercial model depends on gated catalog access, differentiated pricing, and account structure that follows company relationships rather than loose customer grouping alone.

Another important distinction is staged merchandising and content operations.

Adobe Commerce includes Content Staging, which allows business teams to create, preview, and schedule content and merchandising updates directly from the Admin. That changes migration planning because storefront continuity is not only about the static state of pages and catalog rules at launch. It can also depend on how scheduled campaigns, seasonal changes, pricing visibility, and content timing should behave after the move.

Adobe Commerce also carries a broader governance burden. It still uses the websites / stores / store views hierarchy, but in Adobe Commerce that hierarchy often interacts with B2B access rules, customer groups, merchandising control, and staged content in ways that create a larger planning and validation surface than Magento Open Source usually does on its own.

### What Usually Changes in a Migration to Adobe Commerce

A move into Adobe Commerce often changes the store in five important ways.

#### Company structure becomes a primary planning layer

In many Adobe Commerce migrations, the important customer unit can no longer be treated only as the individual customer account.

For many businesses, the meaningful unit is the company and the access rules attached to it. Product visibility, pricing, and purchasing context can all depend on that relationship. A migration can look complete at the record level while still failing to preserve how business customers actually buy.

#### Shared catalogs become a direct control layer for access and pricing

Shared catalogs are not only a B2B feature. They are a structural part of how Adobe Commerce can govern who sees which products and at what price.

That changes migration planning because the target must define which companies should receive which catalog logic, which prices should differ, and whether the resulting commercial structure still reflects the intended business model after launch.

#### Content and merchandising timing become part of the target model

With Content Staging, Adobe Commerce can schedule and preview content and merchandising changes directly in the platform. This makes campaign-aware behavior part of migration thinking.

A business moving into Adobe Commerce should define not only the static storefront state, but also whether important scheduled changes, campaign timing, promotional visibility, or content sequencing still need to work in a governed way after the move.

#### Scope remains important, but its interaction with commercial rules becomes heavier

Adobe Commerce still uses websites, stores, and store views. That hierarchy matters on its own, but in Adobe Commerce it often needs to be read together with customer groups, shared catalogs, pricing visibility, and staged content.

A store can therefore look structurally complete while still being hard to govern if those layers were never designed to work together clearly.

#### Validation burden expands beyond visible storefront checks

Standard storefront validation is not enough for Adobe Commerce. The target often needs to prove company structure, shared catalog behavior, customer-group interaction, route continuity, staged content logic, and higher-value operational scenarios that can appear correct on the surface while behaving incorrectly in practice.

Representative validation is therefore more useful than broad record confirmation alone.

### Where Adobe Commerce Is Often a Strong Target

Adobe Commerce is often a good migration target when a business wants enterprise-native structure, not only richer storefront flexibility.

It is especially well suited to cases where the target must support company accounts, shared-catalog product access, differentiated pricing, broader merchandising control, and more deliberate governance across teams or storefront contexts. It can also fit well when the business needs campaign-aware content and promotional timing to be part of the operating model rather than a loosely managed afterthought.

Adobe Commerce is often strongest when the business can answer questions such as:

* which customers belong to which company structures
* which companies should see which products and prices
* which staged content or merchandising behaviors matter commercially
* which storefront differences belong at website, store, or store-view level
* and which validation scenarios matter most before launch

When those answers are clear, Adobe Commerce can provide a more governed and commercially expressive target than lighter platforms or more loosely assembled enterprise storefronts.

### Where Deeper Planning Is Usually Needed

Adobe Commerce is not automatically the right target just because the business is larger or more operationally complex.

Deeper planning is usually needed when company structure is still vague, when product-access and pricing rules are still described in general terms, when campaign and merchandising timing matter but have not been mapped clearly, or when the business expects the platform’s enterprise capability to resolve ambiguity automatically.

Most Adobe Commerce migration risk is not caused by the inability to move data. It comes from under-defined target behavior. A store can look structurally complete while still failing to preserve who can see what, which prices apply, how customer groups interact with shared catalogs, how staged content should behave, and whether the broader operating model still makes sense after launch.

Adobe Commerce should therefore be approached as a platform of commercial structure, access control, and governed merchandising behavior, not just as a broader version of Magento.

### What Should Be Understood Early Before Moving into Adobe Commerce

Before treating Adobe Commerce as a settled target choice, the business should be able to answer a few important questions clearly.

#### 1. How should company-based customer structure work after the move?

This includes companies, the customer relationships attached to them, and the access rules that should follow that structure.

#### 2. How should shared catalogs control product visibility and pricing?

This is one of the most important target questions because it shapes who can see what and at what commercial terms.

#### 3. Which content or merchandising behavior needs to be staged or scheduled?

Because Adobe Commerce supports Content Staging, campaign-aware behavior should be treated as part of the future-state model where it matters.

#### 4. What should differ by website, store, or store view?

Adobe Commerce still uses the Magento hierarchy, but it often interacts with commercial rules in a heavier way.

#### 5. Which commercial outcomes are non-negotiable?

This includes pricing visibility, product access, customer-group behavior, staged merchandising, and any workflow-critical enterprise behavior that must still work acceptably after launch.

### Conclusion

Adobe Commerce is a strong migration target when the business needs enterprise-native structure for companies, shared catalog access and pricing, staged merchandising behavior, and broader governance than lighter platforms or more loosely assembled storefronts usually carry natively.

Its role is not only to support more capability, but to provide more explicit commercial structure where access, pricing, customer context, and campaign timing must be governed deliberately. Risk increases when teams treat it as Magento plus more features rather than as a platform whose real value sits in clearer company logic, pricing visibility, staged operations, and validation discipline.

### FAQs

#### What makes Adobe Commerce different from Magento in migration planning?

The biggest differences are native B2B company structure, shared catalogs with differentiated pricing and product access, Content Staging, and a broader governance and validation burden around commercial rules.

#### Are shared catalogs only a merchandising feature in Adobe Commerce?

No. Shared catalogs are a direct control layer for product access and pricing visibility for different company contexts.

#### Does Adobe Commerce still use the websites / stores / store views hierarchy?

Yes. Adobe Commerce still uses that hierarchy, but in many projects it interacts more heavily with company structure, shared catalogs, and staged content behavior than it would in Magento Open Source alone.

#### When is Adobe Commerce usually worth considering as a migration target?

Usually when the future store needs native B2B company structure, differentiated catalog and pricing visibility, staged merchandising behavior, and clearer governance over commercial rules than lighter or more loosely assembled targets can support directly.
